Probabilistic risk Assessment (PRA): PRA is essential to keep our power plants, trains and self-driving cars safe and operational. The increased constraints imposed by international standards together with the ever-growing penetration of AI components in high-tech systems make rigorous and powerful risk assessment more important than ever.
Fault trees for PRA: Various techniques have been developed throughout the years to analyse the safety and reliability of systems. One of the most relevant is Fault Tree Analysis (FTA) (Stamatelatos et al., 2002; Ruijters and Stoelinga, 2015). Every day, millions of engineers are using fault trees to model their systems and analyse the probability of failures. FTA is applied to many safety-critical systems and their use is required for instance by the Federal Aviation Authority (FAA), the Nuclear Regulatory Commission (NRC), in ISO 26262 for autonomous driving or for software development in aerospace systems (by NASA and ESA).

Dynamic fault trees: While fault trees are easy to understand and thus, widely used, their modelling capabilities are severely limited. This lack of flexibility hinders accurate and precise modelling of real-life systems. To overcome these drawbacks, several extensions of fault trees have been proposed in the last decades. Dynamic fault trees (DFT) (Dugan et al., 1992) are a prominent extension of fault trees overcoming these limitations. DFTs have been co-developed by NASA to model more complex behaviours and dependability patterns. As such, DFTs can capture more realistic scenarios needed to handle the growing complexity in, e.g., self-driving cars, hyperloops and drones.

Dependability metrics: While fault tree models represent how failures occur at system component level and how they propagate through sub-systems, eventually leading to system level failures, their analysis focuses on computing various dependability metrics, i.e. key performance indicators that measure how well a system performs. Standard metrics are the system:
Reliability : the probability that no failure occurred until time T
Conditional Reliability : the probability that no failure occurred until time T given a component has already failed
Availability : the average percentage of time that a system is operational
Mean Time to Failure (MTTF) : the mean time between failures,
Criticality of components : to what extent does a component failure contribute to a system failure, etc.
Our tools also handles various extensions that include the cost and impact of failures.

Increased modeling power has a price: DFTs are more complex to analyse mainly due to the fact that they incorporate dynamic behaviour, where also the order in which failures occur is important. This significantly increases the number of distinct behaviours which need to be analysed. Various techniques to analyse DFTs proposed before (Tang and Dugan, 2004; Montani et al., 2008; Rauzy, 2011) do not scale to industry-size fault trees, which may comprise hundreds or thousands of fault tree gates. Contrarily, our DFT analysis approach scale provided in multiple industrial-academic projects.

Probabilistic Model-checking: RWTH Aachen University Germany and Twente University the Netherlands have worked on a new fully automated approach to DFT analysis, namely via stochastic model checking.
Stochastic model checking (Baier and Katoen, 2008) is a state-of-the-art analysis technique offering a wide plethora of powerful analysis techniques for stochastic systems. State space reduction techniques, such as symmetry reduction, bisimulation minimization (Baier and Katoen, 2008, Chap. 7), reduce one model to a smaller one that is computationally equivalent. Approximative techniques allow one to approximate analysis results upto arbitrary precision.
Several advanced methods (Ruijters et al., 2016; Ruijters et al., 2017, Volk et al., 2018) have been developed to analyze DFTs that apart from increasing the computational efficiency also provide more modeling power. Further, a wide variety of different metrics are offered, analyzing the system reliability under various scenarios. The combined power of these methods allows us to analyse DFTs that were out of scope before (Katoen and Stoelinga, 2017).
The research results were published in highly ranked journals from both computer science and engineering. For instance, our survey paper on fault trees (Ruijters and Stoelinga, 2015) was cited over 450 times. The publication presenting the underlying model checker (Dehnert et al., 2017) was published in the highest ranked conference in the field of formal verification and cited over 300 times.

Combination of BDD and Markov analysis: Fault trees are a key model in reliability analysis. Classical static fault trees (SFT) can best be analysed using binary decision diagrams (BDD). Whereas state-based techniques are favorable for the more expressive dynamic fault trees (DFT). We combine the best of both worlds by following Dugan’s approach: dynamic sub-trees are analysed via model checking Markov models and replaced by basic events capturing the obtained failure probabilities. The resulting SFT is then analysed via BDDs. Our implementation of Dugan’s approach significantly outperforms pure Markovian analysis of DFTs.
